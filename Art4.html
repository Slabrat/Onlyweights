Highlight: The Deadlift.

Ah. The deadlift. Some have called it the penultimate exercise. The one event where a person lifts more weight and puts more stress on their central nervous system than any other -we are ignoring you, Super-Yoke. There are many who believe that deadlifts should be avoided, that they are dangerous, and unhealthy. Some argument may be made in that regard to folk who perform one repetition max deadlifts, but for the most part, the data I, personally and anecdotally, have seen seems to indicate that it is no more dangerous than any other heavy lift.

But don’t take my word for it. We are about to discover the dead-lift right now. Though there are many schools of thought and eldritch practices for this movement, we will keep it as basic as possible for now.

1 . Stand in front of the bar, feet facing forward and shoulder width. The mid-point of the bar should be right over the center of your feet, or a single inch away from your shins.

2. Grip the bar. There are several grip variations for this. For brevity, and so we can focus on the movement instead of grips, we will use the over hand grip, assisted by lifting straps. Be sure to have the straps emerging from their respective loops in the direction of your thumbs on each hand.

3. Bind your wraps tightly, make sure your hands are equidistant and roughly just outside of shoulder width.

4. Some folks roll the bar out to put it in motion, for this simply take a breath, straighten your legs, beginning already to “pull slack” out of the bar.

5. Once your grip is solidly pulling against the bar lean back and sit, you should feel your grip tight against the bar, a vague sense of getting your weight “under” it.

6. Lift your head, stuffing your arms back and “explode” upward, it should feel like you are pulling with your upper chest. No part of your lower back should move, your hips should be hinging.

6*.Absolutely NONE of the weight should be felt in your arms outside of your grip and the sensation of pushing back with your shoulders/upper arms to keep the bar in its proper bar path.

7. Pause at the top, wait for the coach to give you a nod and then slam the weight to the ground. Or if you lift at a commercial gym painstakingly lower it to the deck.

Programming: A good program for a beginner on deadlift, is to simply lift a single set of six repetitions for each level of weight. When you get to a weight that you can only pull for five repetitions, stop and go do some ham raises. You are done,and ham raises feel amazing after Deadlifts.

***Please do not attempt to do this without a professional to instruct you. I am not a professional, merely a weightlifting enthusiast and this is what works best for me. Welcome to Sports-Entertainment.***
